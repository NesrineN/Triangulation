-cv::SVD::compute(F.t(), W, U, VT); --> performing SVD of F matrix and returning U, W, and V already transposed
Why Transpose F? In Hartley and Sturm's "Optimal Triangulation," they need to find the Epipoles.The left epipole e_1 is the null space of $F$ ($F e_1 = 0$).The right epipole $e_2$ is the null space of $F^T$ ($F^T e_2 = 0$).By computing the SVD of $F^T$, the last column of the matrix $V$ (or the last row of $VT$) gives you the vector that most closely satisfies the equation $F^T x = 0$. This is the standard way to find the epipole in the image.
W: A $3 \times 1$ matrix (the three singular values).U: A $3 \times 3$ orthogonal matrix.VT: A $3 \times 3$ orthogonal matrix.
cv::Mat epipole = VT.row(2);

-cv::SVD::solveZ(F.t(), e2); 
designed to find the unit vector $x$ that is the "best" solution to the homogeneous linear system $Ax = 0$.
The "Z" stands for Zero. In many computer vision problems, you aren't trying to solve $Ax = b$ (where $b$ is some value), but rather $Ax = 0$.
In your case, cv::SVD::solveZ(F.t(), e2); is calculating the right epipole $e_2$ of the Fundamental Matrix $F$.

!!!!!!!!! Vecs are treated as columns !!!!!!!!!!!
If your math equation requires the vector to be a row (horizontal), you have to explicitly tell OpenCV to treat it as a matrix and then transpose it.
However, cv::Vec is a "template," and you cannot directly call .t() on it like a matrix. You usually convert it to a cv::Mat first:
cv::Vec3d v(1, 2, 3); // it's 3x1
cv::Mat rowVec = cv::Mat(v).t(); // Now it is 1x3
